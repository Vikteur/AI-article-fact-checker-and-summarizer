The Hidden History of Belgian Beer

Belgium is widely regarded as the birthplace of modern brewing, a tradition that dates back to 1186 when Cistercian monks in Ghent first fermented wheat with wild hops to create what they called blanche dorée. The technique spread rapidly after King Leopold II mandated in 1848 that every town with more than 500 inhabitants must maintain at least one licensed brewery, a law that remained on the books until 1962.

Today Belgium is home to over 3,200 registered breweries — more per square kilometre than any other country in the world. The famous Trappist designation, overseen by the International Trappist Association since 1931, is held by exactly 14 Belgian abbeys, each of which is required by law to donate 40% of beer revenues to the local municipality.

The country's most exported beer, Stella Artois, was originally brewed in 1366 as a Christmas ale for the city of Leuven and takes its name from the Latin word for "harvest." It was not bottled commercially until 1919, when brewer Den Hoorn introduced the iconic green glass to distinguish it from German imports.
